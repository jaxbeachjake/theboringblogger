Streaming 101 - Part 2

February 19, 2022 in internet, how to

Streaming Caveats

One caveat before introducing Internet-based streaming solutions is to have a talk about bandwidth. This is a measurement of how much data your Internet Service Provider (ISP) allows you to transfer, and what your Internet equipment (both to the ISP, and within your home) is capable of. Usually, when you sign up for Internet service, your monthly fee includes a certain “bandwidth tier”. This could be as low as 6Mbps (Megabytes per second), or as high as 1Gbps (Gigabits per second). Which tier you pay for decides (a), how many Internet connections that can successfully be sustained, and (b) how many (and what types) of streaming you are capable of (think 4k vs standard definition).

Option 2a: Basic Streaming - Smart T.V.’s

If you’ve bought a T.V. within the past 5 years, it’s likely that it’s “smart”. Meaning that it is capable of some kind of network connectivity (either WiFi or via Ethernet cable), and a software package installed with various streaming applications like Netflix or Hulu. Connecting the T.V. to your network will allow these other applications to work. The downside here is that T.V.’s don’t put a lot of resources into great streaming. It is not a primary function of the device, so the quality of the streaming will be limited, which does not reflect on the service itself. So if you are using your T.V. for Netflix, and have poor quality, chances are, the problem isn’t Netflix, but your T.V. or possibly your Internet bandwidth (hint, see the “Streaming Caveats” section above).

Option 2b: Better Quality - Mobile Devices

A standard “smart” phone today packs more technology and resources into it’s small form than it took to send men to the moon years ago…..by quite a lot. And yet this article is focusing on using that technology and power to ensure you don’t miss the latest episode of The Marvelous Mrs. Maisel, LOL. Mobile devices, like your iPhone, and iPAD, or Kindle Fire are great for streaming as well. Especially for discrete scenarios where you don’t want to disturb others. Case-in-point, our son uses his iPad (with BlueTooth headphones), to watch his favorite Netflix shows while he does the dishes. Hey, it’s all about making work fun right?

Option 2c: Purpose-built Equipment

Sometimes it just takes a special tool for a special job. Depending on your needs (and especially if you’re a cord cutter), a separate streaming device is probably right up your alley.
Streaming devices like Roku, Chromecast, and Apple T.V. come in many forms, Roku for example, has several form factors to choose from depending on your needs, that range from small streaming sticks that just hang out of your HDMI port, to full powerhouse bricks that are capable of streaming 4K content.

Streaming devices can get pricey and remember that the price of the device doesn’t always include content. Roku has their channel available for customers that purchase a Roku device, but most people still download applications (“apps”) to the Roku and rely on the device itself for streaming those apps.

The important thing to remember here is that these devices are dynamic content providers, NOT static ones. This is a big difference. This was never made more clearer than with the Roku vs. Google debacle. The short take is that the streaming device (Roku) and Google were renegotiating some contractual agreements, when they came to an impasse. Fingers were pointed, some light accusations of data privacy issues were lobbed, and the result was the YouTube/YouTube T.V. was pulled from availability from Roku for a short time. Existing users could still use the service, but new devices could not download. This continued until they came to an agreement. Until then, the customers were in limbo (myself included).

The takeaway is that these devices perform a service, but are also subject to business decisions and the overall market that could render them (or sub-services they provide) useless at some future point in time, so plan accordingly.

Show Me The Money!

The money savings here resides in the fact that you have control over which streaming service you subscribe to, AND that service usually does not have any kind of contractual agreement. So try out Sling TV, and if you don’t like it, cancel it right away, and move to YouTube T.V., or Hulu. If one of those doesn’t float your boat, cancel them, and try a premium station like HBO Max, or Showtime. I’ve found that for local stations, something like a regular OTA Antenna, or YouTube T.V. works well (at least for me), but as they say on the Internet Your Mileage May Vary (YMMV).

Should I dip my toes in P2P?

If you’re asking about P2P (Point-to-Point), then you’re probably a little more technically inclined that most. However, I would warn just about anyone interested in torrenting movies, showes, games, software, etc. that (1) It’s usually illegal, and (2) The files themselves will normally come packed with viruses and other malware.
Yes, there are safe methods for torrenting, but when considering the ethical implications involved, it is normally not worth the extra headaches and precautions, just to watch a bad shaky camera copy of whatever the latest Spiderman movie is (that you will probably end up going to the movies to see anyway).

Last Thoughts

No matter which methods you use to stream, whether using an OTA antenna, or a Roku device, it offers unparalleled flexibility over your typical cable/satellite provider setup at a much more reasonable cost while letting you make the most of the Internet connection that you are already paying for.
