Streaming 101 - Part 1

February 10, 2022 in internet, how to

Jump In!

The best way I’ve found to learn things in technology is by immersion, so we’ll start with a short intro and then this first part 1 will be focused on saving money with streaming through an old school style HD antenna. In part 2 we’ll dive into Internet streaming services and options.

Why do you want to stream anyway?

The past few years has seen a surge in people cutting the cord. By this I mean people that opt not to pay for cable or satellite television service. The cable and satellite industry offers high prices with little-to-no configuration options. You get some of what you want, but mostly you pay for what you don’t want. On top of that there are extra fees involved including equipment rental fees, bandwidth caps, and yes sometimes streaming service fees.

So what is “cutting the cord?”

On the other hand cutting the cord involves keeping your Internet service, but opting only for the Internet service, and not the extra television components. Sometimes the cable companies make the prices such that you still get the television service, at the same cost you would if you just did the Internet by itself, but this is not always what it appears, as there is usually additional equipment involved that again, comes with its own monthly rental (or other hidden) fees.
So this article will review different options for how to “stream” different Internet-based services to supplement your previous television services.

Option 1: Old School Antenna, No Internet Needed!

Before cable or satellite T.V. television was available via radio waves that the stations transmitted. Remember “bunny ears” on T.V. sets? Or the long T.V. antenna poles that used to be attached to houses? Maybe I’m showing my age here. I was raised in a small Texas town so most everyone had a large antenna attached to their home. Frequencies have been upgraded over the years with the addition of HD (High Definition) for some channels. You don’t necessarily have to have a large antenna attached to your home to benefit here, there are very small HD antennas that can work quite well for most people, although there are some caveats.

Once such caveat is location. Your antenna is a receiver and so you have to live within a certain distance to the transmitter. On top of this, you will want to make sure your antenna is oriented (pointed) toward the transmitter (if possible). Antennas can work inside the house (if smaller, and positioned near a window, and within range), in the attic (if larger and positioned fairly precisely toward the transmitter), or going full old-school by attaching the antenna to the outside of your house (I’ve seen satellite T.V. mounts retro-fitted with a local antenna).

The connection from the antenna to your television is standard coaxial cable. A very basic implementation here would be to connect the antenna to the back of your T.V. (there is usually an “ANT” coax input specifically for this), and selecting a channel scan from your T.V. menu.

It can be hard to find channels in the right range and orientation, so there are websites that can help, assuming you know the “call sign” for the station transmitting the channels you want to receive. One such site is Rabbit Ears.

The problem with local channels is that you don’t automatically have the comforts that cable/satellite or streaming services do, like full program menus and DVR options, BUT there are options for this too!
Meet Tablo, an Over-The-Air (OTA) DVR solution. I’ve had a couple of Tablo devices and can vouch for the device and service. Tablo is an intermediary device that you plug your antenna into, then you can usually use an HDMI cable from Tablo to your T.V. The cool thing about Tablo is that it can create a full program menu (similar to what you have with other services). The real-time menu is free, but a menu containing future items (necessary if you have the DVR option for future program recording) is $5 a month. The DVR option is via local storage.

I don’t personally utilize a local antenna or Tablo solution currently. This is because my wife and I live too far away from reliable transmitters, and it’s too easy for us to utilize streaming for what we need.

Stay tuned for part 2 where we’ll dive into Internet streaming options and services!
